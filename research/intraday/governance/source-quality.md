# Source Quality

## Purpose

Source quality evaluates whether a source can be responsibly used for research extraction. It is separate from the multidimensional evidence profile, convergence, and architecture relevance.

## Review Dimensions

- **Identity:** Is the author, speaker, institution, or venue identifiable?
- **Provenance:** Is the original publication and date available?
- **Primary status:** Is this the originating material or a secondary retelling?
- **Method transparency:** Are data, definitions, samples, and methods disclosed?
- **Scope:** Are instrument, venue, jurisdiction, period, and market conditions clear?
- **Reproducibility:** Could another reviewer inspect or repeat the basis of the claim?
- **Conflict disclosure:** Are commercial, positional, or promotional interests visible?
- **Recency:** Could rules, structure, technology, or market practice have changed?
- **Completeness:** Is material context missing, edited, translated, or caption-generated?
- **Falsifiability:** Does the source state what evidence would contradict the claim?

## Quality Labels

- `HIGH`: primary, identifiable, transparent, scoped, and reproducible.
- `MODERATE`: useful provenance with material method or scope limitations.
- `LOW`: anecdotal, incomplete, opaque, promotional, or difficult to verify.
- `UNASSESSED`: collected but not yet reviewed.

## Rules

- Record source quality in the final research assessment.
- Do not infer missing metadata.
- Auto-generated captions are unverified representations and reduce textual confidence.
- Separate the quality of a source from the correctness of any individual claim.
- Document changes when a live webpage, rule, or institutional publication is versioned.
