## Source Summary

Zalesky presents a behavioral rule set for short-term day trading. He organizes success around three elements: market information, execution mechanics, and discipline. The 25 rules emphasize consistency, rapid exit from adverse trades, smaller size during poor performance, daily loss limits, repeatable methods, trade logging, scaling favourable positions, and avoidance of hesitation, hope, oversized losses, and changing methods from day to day.

The article is direct and operational, but its claims are based on the author's floor-trading experience, students, and personal anecdotes. It supplies no controlled evidence that the entire rule set—or its specific dollar amounts, position sizes, scalping preference, and behavioural absolutes—generalizes across traders, venues, technologies, or market regimes.

## Observed Behaviour

- Most rules are expressed as universal imperatives rather than conditional hypotheses.
- Examples are drawn from futures-floor and early electronic-trading contexts.
- Discipline, profitability, and consistency are sometimes treated as directly equivalent.
- The article mixes governance concepts with specific contracts, dollar limits, lot sizes, targets, and scalping preferences.
- Adverse trades, scratches, favourable trades, and day-level performance are discussed as separate outcomes.

## Speaker Interpretation

For this written source, “speaker” means the author's interpretation.

- Discipline is interpreted as the most important component of trading success.
- Rapid loss recognition is interpreted as more valuable than hoping for recovery.
- Trade size is interpreted as something earned through demonstrated consistency and reduced after poor execution.
- A stable method and written prerequisites are interpreted as protection against impulsive switching.
- Small repeated gains and smaller adverse outcomes are interpreted as superior to seeking occasional large wins.
- Scaling out of favourable positions is interpreted as preferable to scaling out of adverse positions.

## Personal Opinion

- The separation of information, mechanics, and discipline is a useful systems framing, even though it is not validated here.
- Several rules express sound governance questions, but the article overstates causal certainty by equating discipline with profit.
- The instruction to trust a “gut” feeling for a bad trade conflicts with the goal of explicit, auditable decision criteria.
- Claims that news is always obsolete, speculation always loses, or short-term scalping is the answer are unsupported universal conclusions.
- Dollar limits, contract counts, trade-frequency examples, and scaling procedures are source-specific parameters.

## Main Claims

### Claim 1 — Discipline determines consistent performance

- Claim: Following a stable behavioral process on every trade increases consistency and profitability.
- Evidence presented: Author experience and student anecdotes.
- Evidence type: personal experience
- Scope and limitations: No comparison group, methodology definition, or independent performance record.

### Claim 2 — Rapid loss acceptance preserves capital and behaviour

- Claim: Exiting adverse trades quickly prevents one large loss from erasing many smaller gains and damaging confidence.
- Evidence presented: Personal examples and rules.
- Evidence type: practitioner experience
- Scope and limitations: Exit criteria, noise tolerance, costs, and strategy dependence are not analysed.

### Claim 3 — Position size should depend on demonstrated execution quality

- Claim: Traders should reduce size after poor performance and increase only after a sustained successful period.
- Evidence presented: Student progression rules and autobiographical examples.
- Evidence type: personal practice
- Scope and limitations: The source uses arbitrary lot counts and day counts without validation.

### Claim 4 — A repeatable method reduces random behaviour

- Claim: Written trade prerequisites, logs, and a stable methodology reduce hesitation and unstructured switching.
- Evidence presented: Practitioner instruction.
- Evidence type: personal experience
- Scope and limitations: The method itself still requires independent validation; consistency cannot rescue a negative expectancy.

### Claim 5 — Short-term scalping is superior to speculation

- Claim: Frequent small wins and smaller losses improve the probability of a successful day or week.
- Evidence presented: Personal assertion.
- Evidence type: unsupported opinion
- Scope and limitations: Costs, speed, adverse selection, market regime, and alternative holding periods are excluded.

- Execution: mechanics, rapid exit, hesitation, and order-entry error.
- Position management: reducing size, earning larger size, scaling favourable positions, and not adding to losses.
- Psychology: hope, fear, confidence, control, greed, and behavioural repetition.

- News: dismissed broadly by the author; the claim remains unsupported.

## Observable Research Questions

- Can compliance and method expectancy be measured separately?
- Which adverse-trade criteria are explicit enough to execute without hindsight or intuition?
- Does size reduction after an adverse sequence improve risk-adjusted outcomes, behaviour, or neither?
- What information must a trade log preserve to attribute failure to analysis, execution, risk, or management?
- When does scaling out improve exposure control, and when does it reduce positive expectancy?

## Potential KRONOS Relevance

- Decision Audit: distinguish rule compliance from favourable or adverse outcome.
- Execution Governance: record order-entry errors, hesitation, and invalidation response.
- Risk Authority: possible multi-level loss authority without adopting the source's dollar amounts.
- Position Management: separate protective exit authority from favourable-position scaling.
- Session Management: pre-session readiness and day-level stop state may be reviewable responsibilities.

## Key Statements — Paraphrased

- Information, execution mechanics, and discipline are treated as separate requirements.
- Discipline must be observable on every trade rather than claimed in general.
- Size should decrease when execution deteriorates.
- One large loss can erase many small favourable outcomes.
- A written method should precede action.
- Logs should preserve each trade and day-level context.
- Capital preservation keeps future participation possible.
- Losses should be accepted without hope or prayer.
- Consistency is developed through repeated execution of the same process.
- Favourable-position management should not be confused with rescuing an adverse trade.

## Final Research Assessment

- Source credibility: Identifiable experienced practitioner article.

- Architectural usefulness: Moderate for compliance, execution, risk authority, and audit questions.
- Evidence quality: Low; personal experience and prescriptive rules.

- Main limitation: Universal language, parameterized advice, style-specific assumptions, and no independent validation.
