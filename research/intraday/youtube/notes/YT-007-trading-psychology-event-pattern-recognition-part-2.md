## Source Summary

Hougaard argues that chart analysis is vulnerable to confirmation bias and retrospective selection. A pattern can appear persuasive in historical review while being difficult to identify or execute consistently in real time. He rejects certainty and a perfect system, instead framing trading as a sequence of uncertain opportunities whose outcomes depend on the relationship between adverse and favourable results. The presentation relies mainly on personal experience and an anecdote about a low-win-rate fund.

## Main Claims

### Claim 1

- Claim: Traders see charts through existing beliefs and may seek confirming evidence.
- Supporting explanation: Visual perception and retrospective line drawing can make selected patterns appear objective.
- Evidence presented: Visual examples and chart illustrations.
- Evidence type: anecdotal

### Claim 2

- Claim: Historical research can omit failed signals and overstate real-time usability.
- Supporting explanation: Successful examples remain visually salient, while live monitoring and failed breaks complicate execution.
- Evidence presented: Trend-line examples and a session-monitoring calculation.
- Evidence type: personal experience

### Claim 3

- Claim: No perfect system, indicator, or chart pattern exists.
- Supporting explanation: Trading outcomes remain uncertain and signals fail.
- Evidence presented: No independent evidence supplied.
- Evidence type: unsupported opinion

### Claim 4

- Claim: Win rate alone does not determine profitability.
- Supporting explanation: Frequent small adverse outcomes can coexist with fewer sufficiently large favourable outcomes.
- Evidence presented: Anecdote about an unnamed fund using a DeMark-related approach.
- Evidence type: anecdotal

- Claim: Possessing trading rules is insufficient if the trader cannot execute them.
- Supporting explanation: Behaviour under uncertainty can diverge from a stated method.

- Evidence type: personal experience
