# Evidence Classification

## Purpose

Evidence strength records the typical reliability of the evidence form presented. It is not a truth score, source popularity score, or substitute for reviewing method, scope, recency, and conflicts.

## Strength Scale

| Rating | Typical evidence forms | Required caution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `★★★★★` | Exchange rule; academic paper; peer-reviewed research | Confirm applicability, effective date, method, venue, and whether the source actually supports the claim. |
| `★★★★☆` | Institutional publication; documented professional trading-desk practice; exchange education | Separate formal mechanics from educational simplification or institution-specific practice. |
| `★★★☆☆` | Experienced practitioner; verified case study | Require verifiable scope, records, selection criteria, and conflicts of interest. |
| `★★☆☆☆` | Anecdotal account; personal opinion | Treat as hypothesis-generating, not validating. Seek independent evidence. |
| `★☆☆☆☆` | Marketing; sales material; unsupported claim | Do not use for architecture advancement without materially stronger independent evidence. |

## Evidence Types

- Market mechanics
- Empirical data
- Academic evidence
- Exchange rule or formal venue material
- Institutional practice
- Verified case study
- Personal experience
- Anecdotal
- Opinion
- Marketing or sales
- Unsupported claim

## Classification Rules

1. Rate the evidence supporting each claim, not merely the overall source.
2. Record the weakest material dependency when a claim combines several evidence forms.
3. Multiple sources repeating the same unsupported assertion do not increase evidence strength.
4. Multiple transformations of the same underlying data do not automatically constitute independent confirmation.
5. A high rating does not make a claim architecturally relevant.
6. A low rating does not require deletion; it requires proportionate caution and validation.

## Current YouTube Source Treatment

Practitioner presentations generally begin at `★★★☆☆` only when relevant experience is credible and the claim is grounded in that experience. Unsupported universal claims, anecdotes, and promotional statements within the same presentation must be rated separately at `★★☆☆☆` or `★☆☆☆☆`.
